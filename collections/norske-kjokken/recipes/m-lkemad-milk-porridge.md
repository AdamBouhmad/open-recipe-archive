## Ingredients

- Milk
- Water
- Grains or rice
- Butter
- Salt
- Prepared soda (optional)

## Directions

1. In the preparation of porridge, one must ensure that it boils gently, not fiercely, and without interruption.
2. For milk porridge, place a thick sheet iron in the bottom of the porridge pot, and if the cooking boils over and you must place stronger fire underneath, loosen this sheet, whereby the porridge cooks easily.
3. Over an open fire one can scarcely cook milk porridge.
4. If it is a thin pair of pots you must cook in, it is all the more inclined to scorch, and you must then smear it inside with soft butter and cover it with water; a good remedy against getting milk porridge scorched is to put a little prepared soda in the milk when it is put over the fire.
5. It gives absolutely no taste; about a teaspoon is enough for 3 pints milk.
6. The milk must always boil before the grains are added, and the porridge should not be thinned during cooking; hereby it scorches easily.
7. Rice porridge becomes best when you do not stir it, but then you must never neglect to smear the porridge first.
8. When the milk or water is in a hard boil, the grains are added by the side where it boils strongest and the lid is laid on.
9. The fire must be watched with great care, so that toward the end of cooking it will stand almost still 15 minutes or so before serving, stir in the porridge, and you shall have absolutely no fear, if it seems thin; for by boiling the last quarter under constant stirring, it will become proper.
10. Once you have stirred in the porridge, it must be done regularly.
11. In no kind of milk porridge is salt added before it is taken from the fire.
12. For every quart of milk, take a little heaping tablespoon of cornstarch or groats, which is stirred in some of the cold milk; the remainder is put on the fire, and when it boils, the meal is stirred in, and it boils until the milk is thickened.
13. Two egg yolks are stirred with cold sugar, thinned with warm milk, and poured into the porridge and stirred until it begins to boil; then it is poured into the tureen in which is a little salt and a couple drops vanilla; it tastes good instead of vanilla to boil a couple sticks of hot cinnamon in the milk.
14. You can place boiled eggs cut in half in the tureen; in the milk in which they boil, there must be sugar and a little cinnamon.
15. This milk is then strained and added to the remaining milk.
16. The soup is eaten with small rusks or baked bread, and one usually reckons a quart of milk for 3 persons.
