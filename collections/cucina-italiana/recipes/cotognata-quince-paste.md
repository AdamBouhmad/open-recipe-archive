## Ingredients

- Quinces, 3 kilograms
- Fine white sugar, 2 kilograms

## Directions

1. Put the quinces on the fire covered with water and when they begin to crack, remove them, peel them and scrape them as best you can to remove all the pulp, then pass it through a sieve.
2. Put it back on the fire with the sugar and stir it constantly so it doesn't stick.
3. Seven or eight minutes of boiling will be enough; but then, if taken up with a spoon it begins to fall in shreds, remove it.
4. If you put it in vessels it can serve you as a preserve and made this way it will remain whiter than the one I will describe at No. 741, but with less fragrance, because part of the particular odor of this fruit is lost in the water.
5. To reduce it to quince paste, spread it on a board about the thickness of a coin and dry it in the sun covered with a veil because flies and wasps are very fond of it.
6. When it is dry on top, cut it in the form of chocolate tablets and by passing a knife under it to detach it from the board, turn it over on the opposite side.
7. If you would like to give it bizarre forms, procure some tin molds empty on both sides, fill them, smooth them and detaching the marmalade from the edges carefully, place it equally on the board and dry it in the same manner.
8. You can also coat it with sugar if you wish, and then put 100 grams of white sugar to melt with two spoonfuls of water and when it has boiled enough to form a thread (taking a drop between two fingers) spread each piece with a brush.
9. If the sugar hardens during the operation (which is best to do on a not humid day) put it back on the fire with another drop of water and boil it again.
10. When the sugar is dry on one side and on the edges, spread the opposite side.
