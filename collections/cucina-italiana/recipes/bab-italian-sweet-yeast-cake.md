## Ingredients

- Hungarian flour or finest flour, 250 grams
- Butter, 70 grams
- Powdered sugar, 50 grams
- Sultana raisins, also called currants, 50 grams
- Malaga raisins with seeds removed, 30 grams
- Beer yeast, 30 grams
- Milk or, better, cream, approximately 1 deciliter
- Eggs, 2 whole and 1 yolk
- Marsala, 1 tablespoon
- Rum or cognac, 1 tablespoon
- Candied fruit cut into strips, 10 grams
- Salt, a pinch
- Vanilla flavoring

## Directions

1. Mix the beer yeast with a quarter of the flour and a little of the lukewarm milk, and form a dough of appropriate consistency. Make a cross cut in the dough with a knife so that it shows the necessary swelling in time. Allow it to rise near the fire at very moderate heat, in a covered vessel with a drop of milk, which will take about half an hour.
2. While it rises, beat the eggs in a bowl with the sugar; then add the remaining flour, the risen small dough, the melted and lukewarm butter, the marsala and rum, and if the dough becomes too stiff, soften it with lukewarm milk. Work it thoroughly with a spoon until the mixture detaches from the bowl.
3. Finally add the raisins and candied fruit, and allow it to rise. When it has puffed up, stir it slightly with a spoon and pour it into a buttered mold dusted with powdered sugar mixed with flour.
4. The best mold form for this cake is copper with ribs; but note that it must be twice as large as the contents. Cover it with a lid so it does not get air and place it in a warm bath or inside a country oven, very slightly hot, to rise it; this will take perhaps more than two hours. If the rising is perfect you will see the mixture grow to double, that is, reach the mouth of the mold. Then bake it, taking care that it does not get air in the meantime. Doneness is tested by inserting a pomegranate stick which should come out dry; nevertheless leave it to dry further in the oven at moderate heat, which is necessary because of its thickness.
