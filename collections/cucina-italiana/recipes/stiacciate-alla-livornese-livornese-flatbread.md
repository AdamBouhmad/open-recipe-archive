## Ingredients

- brewer's yeast
- water
- flour
- egg
- oil
- sugar
- wine
- butter
- orange flower water
- egg yolk

## Directions

1. Dissolve the brewer's yeast with half a glass of lukewarm water, adding enough flour to form a dough of proper consistency, which you will place on the mound of flour within a bowl, covering it with a layer of the same flour. Keep the bowl protected from air and in the kitchen, or in the warmest place in your house if you have one.
2. In the morning, when the said dough has risen well, place it on the work surface, flatten it and remix it with one egg, one tablespoon of oil, one of sugar, one of wine and enough flour to form once again a larger dough, mixing everything well without working it too much. Replace it on the flour and cover it as before.
3. After six or seven hours, which will be needed for the dough to rise again, add three eggs, three tablespoons of oil, three of sugar, three of wine, and enough flour to form the usual dough and let it rise again, proceeding always in the same manner. To know the point of fermentation, calculate that the dough must increase about three times in volume.
4. Add five eggs, five tablespoons of sugar, five of oil, five of wine and the necessary flour.
5. Add the three remaining eggs and everything else, melting the butter over heat, and mix very well to make the dough homogeneous. If the dough becomes somewhat soft, which is unlikely, add more flour to bring it to the proper consistency.
6. Divide it into three or four parts forming balls and place each one in a baking pan on a sheet of paper that extends beyond the rim, greased with butter, where it lies flat; and since as the dose of ingredients increases, fermentation is slower, the last time, if you wish to hasten it, place the flatbreads to rise in a warm place and when they are well puffed and trembling, brush them with a brush first dipped in orange flower water, then in egg yolk. Bake them in the oven at a very moderate temperature, noting that this last part is the most important and difficult because, being large in volume, there is the chance that strong heat reaches the surface immediately, and the interior remains soft.
