## Ingredients

- Fine flour or Hungarian flour, 200 grams
- Butter, 150 grams
- Or alternatively: flour, 300 grams
- Butter, 200 grams
- Hot water (not boiling)
- Salt to taste
- One spoonful of aquavit
- Butter the size of a walnut

## Directions

1. In winter, mix the flour with hot water that is not boiling, salt to taste, one spoonful of aquavit and butter the size of a walnut, taken from the aforementioned 150 or 200 grams. Once you have formed a dough that is neither too firm nor too soft, work it extensively, at least half an hour, first with your hands, then by throwing it forcefully against the work surface. Shape it into a rectangular loaf, wrap it in a kitchen cloth and let it rest briefly.
2. In the meantime, work the butter, if it is firm, with a hand dampened in water on the work surface to make it smooth and uniformly tender throughout. Then shape it into a loaf like that of the flour and place it in a bowl of fresh water. When the dough has rested, remove the butter from the water, dry it with a cloth and dust it thoroughly with flour.
3. Roll out the dough with a rolling pin only as much as necessary to enclose the butter loaf. Place the butter in the center and fold the edges of the dough over it, joining them together with your fingers and ensuring it adheres to the butter in all parts so no air remains between them.
4. Now begin to roll it out first with your hands, then with the rolling pin, thinning it out the first time as much as you can, being careful that the butter does not burst through. If this happens, immediately sprinkle a bit of flour where the butter appears, and frequently dust flour on the work surface and rolling pin so that the dough slides and stretches beneath it.
5. After the first rolling, fold the dough in three, like three overlapping sheets, and roll it out again to a reasonable thickness. Repeat this operation six times in total, letting the dough rest for ten minutes from time to time. On the last, which would be the seventh, fold it in two and reduce it to the thickness needed, that is something less than one centimeter.
6. Except for this last folding, make sure to give the dough a rectangular shape each time you stretch it, three times longer than it is wide, and if air pockets appear, prick them with a needle.
7. A marble slab would be better than a common work surface as it is colder and smoother. In summer, ice is necessary, both to firm up the butter before using it and to stretch the dough better, which is achieved by passing ice over the dough when necessary, wrapped in a very tight kitchen cloth or, better, placed between two plates covered with ice.
8. With puff pastry, as you know, one makes vol-au-vent, small pastries with jam or preserves, and pies filled with marzipan. If you wish to use the pastries as an intermediate course, fill them with a delicate mixture of meat, liver and sweetbreads. But in all ways these pastries should be glazed with egg yolk on the surface but not on the edges to avoid preventing puffing. If they are for dessert, dust them while hot with powdered sugar.
