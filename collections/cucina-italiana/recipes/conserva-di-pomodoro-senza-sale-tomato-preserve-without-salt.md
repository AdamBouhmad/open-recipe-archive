## Ingredients

- Field tomatoes
- Copper pot (not tinned)

## Directions

1. Choose field tomatoes rather than garden tomatoes, as they are less watery, and prefer small ones to large ones.
2. Crush them roughly and put them in a copper pot (not tinned) over a wood fire. Do not worry about the acid attacking the copper when it is on the fire and retains the heat of boiling.
3. When they are cooked and broken down, pour them into a tightly-woven sack held suspended and, once the water has drained, pass them through a strainer to clean them of seeds and skins, squeezing them well.
4. Wash the pot carefully and return them to the fire to reduce them as needed. To know the precise point of consistency the preserve should have, pour a few drops onto a plate and if you see that it does not run and does not present watery seroseness around it, that will mean this is the correct point of cooking.
5. Then bottle it, and you will have another proof of its sufficient density if you see it descend with difficulty through the funnel.
6. Prefer small bottles to consume them quickly; they can remain unopened for 12 or 13 days without the preserve suffering. Use white bottles that come in commerce with Recoaro water, or in their absence, half-bottles of dark beer. Seal them with cork stoppers put on by hand, but which seal well, and tie them with twine, taking care to leave a little air between the stopper and the liquid.
7. Place the said bottles in a pot amongst hay, rags, or similar things so that they stand tightly together, and pour into the pot enough water that reaches up to the neck of the bottles and heat from underneath. Watch as soon the stopper of the bottles will show signs of rising and spraying away if it were not tied, and then stop the fire, because the operation is truly finished.
8. Remove the bottles when the water is cold or even before, replace with a finger the displaced stoppers to put them back in place and keep the bottles in the cellar.
