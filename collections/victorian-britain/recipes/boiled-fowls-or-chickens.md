## Ingredients

- A pair of fowls
- water

## Directions

1. In choosing fowls for boiling, those that are not black-legged are generally much whiter when dressed.
2. Pick, draw, singe, wash, and truss them as follows: Cut off the neck, leaving sufficient skin to skewer back.
3. Cut the feet off to the first joint, tuck the stumps into a slit made on each side of the belly.
4. Twist the wings over the back of the fowl, and secure the top of the leg and the bottom of the wing together by running a skewer through them and the body.
5. Do the other side in the same manner.
6. Should the fowl be very large and old, draw the sinews of the legs before tucking them in.
7. Make a slit in the apron of the fowl, large enough to admit the parson's nose, and tie a string on the tops of the legs to keep them in their proper place.
8. When they are firmly trussed, put them into a stewpan with plenty of hot water.
9. Bring it to boil, and carefully remove all the scum as it rises.
10. Simmer very gently until the fowl is tender; the slower it boils, the plumper and whiter will the fowl be.
11. Many cooks wrap them in a floured cloth to preserve the colour, and to prevent the scum from clinging to them; in this case, a few slices of lemon should be placed on the breasts, over these a sheet of buttered paper, and then the cloth.
12. Boiled ham, bacon, boiled tongue, or pickled pork are the usual accompaniments to boiled fowls, and they may be served with Béchamel, white sauce, parsley and butter, oyster, lemon, liver, celery, or mushroom sauce.
13. Pour a little sauce over the fowls after the skewers are removed, and send the remainder in a tureen to table.
14. Large fowl, 1 hour; moderate-sized one, 3/4 hour; chicken, from 20 minutes to 1/2 hour.
