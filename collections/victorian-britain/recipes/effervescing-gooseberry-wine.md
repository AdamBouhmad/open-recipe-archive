## Ingredients

- To every gallon of water allow 6 lbs. of green gooseberries
- 3 lbs. of lump sugar

## Directions

1. Select unripe gooseberries that have nearly attained their full growth, before they show any tendency to ripen
2. Reject any bruised or decayed berries, and those that are very small
3. Remove the blossom and stalk ends
4. Well bruise the fruit in a tub or pan, in such quantities as to insure each berry being broken without crushing the seeds
5. Pour the warm water on the fruit, squeeze and stir it with the hand until all the pulp is removed from the skin and seeds
6. Cover the whole closely for 24 hours
7. Strain it through a coarse bag, and press it with as much force as can be conveniently applied, to extract the whole of the juice and liquor the fruit may contain
8. To every 40 or 50 lbs. of fruit one gallon more of hot water may be passed through the marc, or husks, in order to obtain any soluble matter that may remain, and be again pressed
9. Put the juice into a tub or pan of sufficient size to contain all of it, and add the sugar to it
10. Well stir until the sugar is dissolved, and place the pan in a warm situation
11. Keep it closely covered, and let it ferment for a day or two
12. Draw off into clean casks, placed a little on one side for the scum that arises to be thrown out
13. Keep the casks filled with the remaining must that should be reserved for that purpose
14. When the active fermentation has ceased, plug the casks upright, fill again if necessary, put in the bungs loosely
15. After a few days, when the fermentation is a little more languid, drive in the bungs tight, and make a spile-hole to give vent if necessary
16. About November or December, on a clear fine day, rack the wine from its lees into clean casks, which may be rinsed with brandy
17. After a month, examine to see if it is sufficiently clear for bottling; if not, fine with isinglass, which may be dissolved in some of the wine: 1 oz. will be sufficient for 9 gallons
18. In March or April, or when the gooseberry bushes begin to blossom, bottle the wine, in order to insure its being effervescing
