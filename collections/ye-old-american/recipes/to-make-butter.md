## Ingredients

- milk
- cream
- salt

## Directions

1. Scald your milk pans every day after washing them; and let them set till the water gets cold.
2. Wipe them with a clean cloth.
3. Fill them all with cold water half an hour before milking time, and do not pour it out till the moment before you are ready to use the pans.
4. Strain the milk into the pans, which should be broad and shallow.
5. Place them in the spring-house, setting them down in the water.
6. After the milk has stood twenty-four hours, skim off the cream, and deposite it in a large deep earthen jar, commonly called a crock, which must be kept closely covered.
7. Stir up the cream with a stick at least twice a day, and whenever you add fresh cream to it.
8. Strain the cream from the crock into the churn, and put on the lid.
9. Move the handle slowly in warm weather, as churning too fast will make the butter soft.
10. When you find that the handle moves heavily and with great difficulty, the butter has come.
11. Take it out with a wooden ladle, and put it into a small tub or pail.
12. Squeeze and press it hard with the ladle, to get out all that remains of the milk.
13. Add a little salt, and then squeeze and work it for a long time.
14. Set it away in a cool place for three hours, and then work it over again.
15. Wash it in cold water; weigh it; make it up into separate pounds, smoothing and shaping it.
16. Clap each pound on your wooden butter print, dipping the print every time in cold water.
17. Spread a clean linen cloth on a bench in the spring-house; place the butter on it, and let it set till it becomes perfectly hard.
18. Wrap each pound in a separate piece of linen that has been dipped in cold water.
