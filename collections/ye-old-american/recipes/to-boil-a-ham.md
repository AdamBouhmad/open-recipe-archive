## Ingredients

- ham
- cold water
- egg
- bread-raspings
- writing paper

## Directions

1. Soak the ham in water previous to boiling to draw out a portion of the salt and make it tender.
2. If a new ham not very salt or hard, soak it in lukewarm water till the evening before cooking.
3. An older ham will require twenty-four hours' soaking.
4. A very old and hard ham should be kept in soak two or three days, frequently changing the soft water.
5. When ready to cook, scrape and trim it nicely, and pare off all the rough-looking parts.
6. Early in the morning put it into a large pot or kettle with plenty of cold water.
7. Place it over a slow fire that it may heat gradually; it should not come to a boil in less than an hour and a half, or two hours.
8. When it boils, quicken the fire, and skim the pot carefully.
9. Simmer it gently four or five hours or more, according to its size; a ham weighing fifteen pounds should simmer five hours after it has come to a boil.
10. Keep the pot well skimmed.
11. When done, take it up and carefully strip off the skin, and reserve it.
12. Rub the ham all over with some beaten egg, and strew on it fine bread-raspings shaken through the lid of a dredging box.
13. Place it in an oven to brown and crisp, or on a hot dish set over the pot before the fire.
14. Cut writing paper into a handsome fringe, and twist it round the shank-bone.
15. Garnish the edge of the dish with little piles or spots of rasped crust of bread.
16. When you put it away after dinner, skewer on again the skin.
