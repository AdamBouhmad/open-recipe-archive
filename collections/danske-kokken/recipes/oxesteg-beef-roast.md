## Ingredients

- Beef, preferably from a young, well-fed animal
- Salt
- Butter, the best quality fresh butter
- Bouillon, ale, or water (optional, used sparingly)
- Butcher's paper, buttered

## Directions

1. Select a piece of meat from a young, well-fed animal, recognizable by its beautiful red color (not dark red), fine fat threads throughout, and white and clear bone. Prefer the tenderloin, though the thick roast, brisket, or leg and neck can also be used if the animal was young and fat.
2. Allow the meat to hang for several days, according to the season, before roasting.
3. Calculate about half a pound of roast per person, preferring a larger piece to a smaller one.
4. Heat the oven appropriately according to the size of the piece to be roasted, so that the pores in the meat contract and it retains its juices.
5. Scatter salt in the pan to prevent the roast from sticking.
6. Place the roast in the pan with the best side up, which should also be uppermost when serving. Never turn the roast during roasting.
7. If the meat is very fatty, it will soon melt enough fat to provide sufficient liquid; otherwise, add a little butter to the pan.
8. Only if there is danger that the butter or fat will become too brown, pour a little bouillon, ale, or water under the roast, but only sparingly, as much liquid produces a poor roast that seems boiled.
9. After the roast has been in the oven for half an hour, drip it with the sauce from the pan, or pour new butter over it.
10. Continue to drip the roast from time to time, but do not keep the oven door open too long.
11. If the roast threatens to become too brown, place a piece of buttered butcher's paper over it, lifting it each time the roast is to be dripped.
12. Roast mainly at brisk heat in the beginning; towards the end, the heat may be reduced somewhat.
13. When done, the roast should be red inside but not bloody, and the juice should flow out when cut. A 12-pound roast requires approximately 2½ hours, and one generally allows a quarter hour more for each additional 2 pounds.
14. When the roast is removed from the oven and placed on the serving dish, pour all the juice that has run into the pan into a pot along with the scrapings from the pan.
15. Boil it with thin soup or gravy and water, strain it, skim the fat, brown butter very lightly, stir a little flour into it, thin it with the roasting gravy, and give it all a quick boil.
16. Serve the roast with this sauce, along with raw-peeled, boiled, or browned potatoes, and according to the season with salad, cucumbers, aspics, etc.
17. Provide very hot plates for this and all roasts.
