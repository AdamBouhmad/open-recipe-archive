## Ingredients

- Mutton
- Salt
- Sugar
- Saltpeter (potassium nitrate)
- Water
- Juniper twigs

## Directions

1. For salting only a smaller supply of meat, Höganäs jars (glazed inside and outside) can be used to advantage. For salting larger supplies, wooden vessels are used. The best are those made of oak or ash, but vessels of pine can also be used. Before use, wooden vessels should be boiled out with juniper twigs. The brine is made from water and juniper twigs and is left in the vessel, which should be filled to the brim. Let the brine stand until it has cooled; it is then poured out and the vessel is dried well. Rub the bottom and sides with salt and place a fairly thick layer of coarse salt on the bottom.
2. Now cut the meat. First cut off steaks and shoulder pieces, then cut the rest of the meat into appropriately sized pieces. Remove all glands and rub the meat well, particularly near the bones, with a mixture of salt, sugar, and saltpeter. For 2 kilograms of meat calculate: 6 tablespoons coarse salt, 3 tablespoons sugar, 1.5 teaspoons potassium nitrate. The saltpeter prevents the red meat color from being lost during salting. Saltpeter does make the meat tough, but this is counteracted by the sugar.
3. Place the meat in the prepared vessel, alternating with salt. Use coarse salt for this, as it contains more salt than fine salt. Place the skin sides down and the meat sides against each other. At the bottom, place steaks and shoulder pieces with the meat sides in and as tightly together as possible. Fill all small holes and cavities with salt, and place salt between the meat pieces so that they do not touch directly.
