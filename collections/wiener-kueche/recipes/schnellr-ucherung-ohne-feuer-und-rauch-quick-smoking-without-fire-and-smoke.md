## Ingredients

- All meat must be completely fresh
- For veal: saltpeter, cooking salt, crushed juniper berries, chopped garlic, rose geranium leaves, lemon peel with white pith, bay leaves
- Rain water or boiled well water
- For the smoking process: 2 pounds gloss soot (chimney soot) from a chimney where only wood is burned, 6 measures (18 apothecary pounds) rain or boiled well water, 8 pounds cooking salt
- For finishing: pure distilled wood vinegar

## Directions

1. Ensure all meat is completely fresh; for veal, remove from hide immediately after slaughtering to prevent unpleasant odors
2. Wash the veal meat clean and rub it with some saltpeter, then let it lie for four hours in a cool place in an appropriate oak or stone vessel without pressure
3. Dissolve cooking salt in warm rain water or boiled well water
4. Rub the meat firmly with crushed juniper berries, sprinkle with finely chopped garlic and some rose geranium leaves, lemon peels with white pith, and bay leaves
5. Ensure the meat forms no hollow spaces in its layers; fill any gaps with small pieces of the same meat to enable even pressure
6. Cover the meat with the cooled salt brine and let it stand for three days in a dry cellar, covered only with a cloth to protect from dirt, without any pressure
7. On the fourth day, load the meat with stones and increase pressure in winter until the twelfth day, then remove the stones; after two days, apply the previous pressure again for four days
8. For veal, the proper time in brine is eight to twelve days in summer, fourteen to eighteen days in winter
9. For beef and pork, use 14 pounds salt and 2 pounds saltpeter per 25 pounds meat; let it lie 24 days in winter or 14-18 days in summer
10. For poultry and fish, rub salt in the cavity (and some pepper may be used) but do not apply pressure
11. Approximately 60 hours before removing the meat from the salt brine, prepare 2 pounds of clean gloss soot by grinding it to fine powder
12. Pour 6 measures (18 apothecary pounds) of rain or boiled well water over the soot, cover the vessel, and stir the prepared brine frequently
13. Before the last two hours pass, add eight pounds of cooking salt to this soot brine and stir well
14. Remove the meat from the salt brine, let it drain, place it in another clean vessel, and cover it with the soot brine
15. If the brine is insufficient, mix the remaining sediment with the required amount of boiled water and add it
16. Let the treated meat lie for 5 to 6 hours depending on its diameter, tilting the vessel frequently
17. Remove the meat from the soot brine and hang it in an airy place, avoiding contact with walls, to dry completely
18. Finally, brush it with a brush using pure distilled, clarified wood vinegar (though it can be used immediately without this step)
19. Note that the meat must not remain too long in the gloss soot brine, as it would otherwise acquire an unpleasant pitch odor
20. For beef and pork, prepare the gloss soot brine from 6 pounds cooking salt, 1 pound pure gloss soot, and 5 measures water; weight these meats with stones and let them lie 8 to 9 hours, frequently turning the pieces and stirring the brine well
21. Poultry and fish remain in the veal's gloss soot brine for 1 to 2 hours depending on their size
22. All meat pieces must have loops attached before salting for later hanging and drying
23. All types of sausages made from fresh meat and fat of completely healthy animals, without excessive thinning of the sausage mass by meat broth or other liquids promoting souring, can be smoked without prior salting by lying 3 hours in the described gloss soot brine in a manner completely harmless to health; they should hang in the air at least 48 hours
