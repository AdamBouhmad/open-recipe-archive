## Ingredients

- One substantial entrecote steak, meat aged four days in winter or two days in summer
- Pork lard

## Directions

1. Have a very hot fire ready.
2. Strike the entrecote on both sides with a boxwood mallet on a wooden cutting board, then clean it and remove all attached fats, skin, nerves, and tendons.
3. Trim the meat so that only uniform, solid meat remains, with no trace of anything else—this is the secret to success.
4. Put enough pork lard in a pan so that when melted, the entrecote can be submerged two or three times without the fat stewing or startling it, but only to completely impregnate it with lard.
5. Remove the entrecote from the pan with an iron fork, holding it suspended in the air so the fat drips onto a plate.
6. Empty most of the fat from the pan, leaving it only lightly greased, and place it on very hot fire until the small amount of remaining lard crackles.
7. Using the same fork, place the entrecote in the pan, shaking the pan slightly.
8. After two or three minutes, small air bubbles will appear on the surface of the meat and burst. Then sprinkle the entrecote with ground salt—nothing but salt—and turn it over.
9. The same bubbling effect appears on the second surface, and complete the seasoning with another dusting of salt.
10. Remove the pan far from the fire and cover it hermetically with a fitted lid and weight on top.
11. After five minutes, serve it alone on a very hot plate.
12. Before cutting the entrecote, you will see it bathed in abundant juice according to this method, and the meat will be as tender as sponge cake.
