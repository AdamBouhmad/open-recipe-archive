## Ingredients

- good or poor fruit
- green or rotten fruit
- warm water
- honey (optional)

## Directions

1. Crush the fruit designated for vinegar production in large containers and pour warm—but not boiling—water over it; do not use too much water as the vinegar would be very weak.
2. Let the pulp made this way lie in special containers for two to three weeks, stirring well several times daily so that the layers work through completely.
3. After this time, press the pulp thoroughly, and transfer the obtained liquid into barrels and containers in a warm room, in such a way that dust and other impurities cannot contaminate it.
4. Cover these containers with light cloth for this purpose.
5. The warmer the room in which the expressed liquid is stored, the faster the vinegar will develop, as heat strongly promotes fermentation and all kinds of decomposition.
6. To obtain vinegar quite quickly, it is good to warm a portion of the pulp over moderate heat every few days and pour the warmed portion into the barrel, which greatly speeds up and promotes fermentation.
7. The warming must not occur rapidly and too strongly, as boiling damages the fruit liquid and one would wait in vain for good vinegar.
8. To make the fruit vinegar have particularly delicate taste and beautiful color, it is good to add a little honey to the fruit liquid, preferably adding the honey always to that fruit liquid which is being warmed.
9. During fermentation the fruit liquid throws off a quantity of impurities, which can be removed several times daily with a flat perforated spoon, and this is repeated until the liquid becomes completely clear and no longer clouds, nor throws off any impurities.
10. As soon as the liquid has cleared, it gradually transforms into vinegar, which soon acquires a pleasant sharpness and tartness, and then it is time to draw it off and decant it into good, odor-free and clean barrels, and the decanted vinegar is stored in a dry, well-ventilated and also cold room, because in heat it readily spoils.
