## Ingredients

- a shin of beef
- one good-sized chicken
- sweet herbs
- mixed vegetables
- six snipes
- two glasses of port wine
- a dessert-spoonful of red currant jelly
- a dessert-spoonful of crystal-vinegar
- a couple of ounces of butter
- a tablespoonful of flour
- a glass of port
- a tea-spoonful of red currant jelly
- juice of a good lime
- a raw egg

## Directions

1. Make a good strong stock for six basins (at least a quart and a half) with a shin of beef, and one good-sized chicken cut up into fragments; flavour it highly with sweet herbs, and mixed vegetables.
2. Take six snipes, and lightly roast them: pick the whole of the meat from their breasts, and save it; mash the bones that remain, and put them in a bowl with the trails.
3. When the stock is quite ready, strain it, return it to the pot, and throw into it the bowlful of mashed snipe bones, two glasses of port wine, a dessert-spoonful of red currant jelly, and a dessert-spoonful of crystal-vinegar.
4. Let the contents of the pot simmer slowly for an hour and half, skimming off any scum that may rise.
5. Strain the liquor from the bones, and set it in a bowl to cool.
6. Pass the snipe meat that you saved through the mincing machine, and pound it thoroughly to a paste, using a little of the soup to help that operation.
7. Skim any fat that may have risen on the surface of your soup.
8. Take a roomy sauce-pan, place it on the fire, put a couple of ounces of butter into it, stir into the butter a tablespoonful of flour, work it to a smooth paste.
9. Add, by degrees both soup and snipe paste, keeping the spoon at work the whole time.
10. When all is expended, let the puree reach the boil in order to thicken properly.
11. Add a glass of port, a tea-spoonful of red currant jelly, and the juice of a good lime.
12. Break a raw egg into a cup, saving the yolk carefully; mix a little of the soup with the yolk.
